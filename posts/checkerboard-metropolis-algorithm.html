The Checkerboard Metropolis Algorithm Explained

July 28, 2023 • Ising Model, Physics, GPU, Parallel Computing

In statistical physics, the Ising model represents magnets through a lattice of spins where each spin can be +1 or -1. The model exhibits a phase transition in 2D near the critical temperature T_c = 2.269, as shown analytically by Onsager.

Monte Carlo simulation is a practical way to study this system numerically. A common approach is the Metropolis algorithm, which proposes spin flips and accepts or rejects them based on the energy difference and temperature.

Why Checkerboard Updates?

A basic Metropolis update flips one spin at a time, which can become computationally expensive for large lattices. Parallelizing spin updates is challenging because neighboring spins are interdependent.

The checkerboard method resolves this by splitting the lattice into two non-interacting color groups (black and white), then updating one color at a time using values from the opposite color. This allows safe parallel updates.

    return lattice

Result Summary

Parallel checkerboard simulation substantially improves throughput across most lattice sizes. For some small cases, overhead can reduce gains, but at larger scales the parallel approach and GPU acceleration provide clear performance advantages.
